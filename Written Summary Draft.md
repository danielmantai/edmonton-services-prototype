## Section 1: Leverage Areas

This prototype grew from systems work done across Weeks 3 through 9. The leverage analysis drew on our iceberg model, causal loop diagram, and an interview with Matt, a former inner-city outreach worker.

We identified leverage areas at three levels.

**High leverage (mental models and system goals):** The deepest drivers of the homelessness system are the mental models underneath it. Housing treated as a commodity rather than a human right shapes every policy and funding decision downstream. Addiction treated as a moral failure rather than a health condition determines who gets funded to respond and how. And measuring success by shelter beds filled rather than people housed permanently means the system optimizes for the wrong outcome. Shifting any of these mental models would change the system fundamentally, but the work required is long-term and political.

**Medium leverage (information flows and system structure):** The service system prioritizes delivery over accessibility. Many supports exist, but people cannot find or reach them. Services are fragmented across dozens of organizations, each with its own intake process. People rely on word-of-mouth and trial and error instead of reliable information. Users may lack phones or internet access, making digital-only solutions insufficient. Within this fragmentation, identification requirements create a hard barrier at the entry point of nearly every service beyond emergency shelter (Homeless Hub, 2017; Government of Alberta, n.d.).

**Low leverage (parameters):** Adding shelter beds does not fix the system. Adjusting operating hours and intake policies produces marginal improvements. These are the most visible interventions and the least effective at scale.

**Selected leverage area:** Fragmentation of services and difficulty navigating the system is the most critical leverage point. It creates barriers in awareness, identification, and access to the right support at the right time. Matt described a landscape where "each organization does specific good work but operates in isolation" (personal communication, March 12, 2026). Within this broader fragmentation problem, the loss and absence of identification documents stood out as a particularly concrete, addressable barrier. Individuals cycle through obtaining ID, experiencing violence or displacement, losing everything, and restarting from zero. The system does not account for this loop. Our prototype targets that loop directly.

## Section 3: How Might We Question

**Selected HMW:** How might we improve access to essential services for individuals experiencing homelessness so they can more easily navigate support systems and improve their quality of life?

This HMW was developed through group consensus. We rejected framings that were too abstract (mental health outcomes for youth) and framings too narrow to generate useful prototypes. The selected HMW is broad enough to explore navigation tools, ID solutions, and coordination systems, while staying grounded in the core barrier of service accessibility.

**What do we need to provide?**
- Easier access to essential services (shelter, food, healthcare, support programs)
- Clear and centralized information about available services
- Coordinated communication between service providers
- Reduced barriers to access (identification requirements, transportation, awareness)
- Ability to verify identity or eligibility without traditional ID
- Continuity of intake records so people do not restart from zero at each organization

**For whom?**
- Individuals experiencing homelessness, especially those without government-issued ID
- People with limited time, transportation, or mobility
- People experiencing mental health or addiction challenges
- Individuals balancing survival needs with the work of accessing services

**Why?**
- Improve access to basic needs and funded programs
- Reduce time and frustration navigating a fragmented system
- Increase consistency and reliability of support
- Build documentation trails that support housing applications

## Section 4: Brainstorming

Our group generated 22 ideas across two rounds of ideation. We followed design thinking rules: defer judgment, quantity over quality, encourage unusual ideas, build on each other.

**Round 1 (individual ideation):**

*Navigation and access:*
1. Real-time service availability map showing live shelter space, food bank capacity, and clinic hours.
2. Service navigator: assign individuals a dedicated guide to help them navigate services.
3. One-stop service hub with all essential services accessible in one place or platform.
4. SMS-based service directory that works without a smartphone.
5. AI chatbot that guides users through services step-by-step.
6. Warm referral system: instead of handing someone a phone number, the current worker calls ahead and confirms the next appointment.
7. Outreach teams deployed on-site when encampments are cleared, to connect people to housing and intake immediately.

*Coordination and awareness:*
8. Centralized, always-updated service database shared by all providers.

*Accessibility:*
9. Drop-in service days where multiple providers are available in one place at the same time.
10. Extended service hours on evenings and weekends.

*Barrier reduction:*
11. Shelter-network photo ID card with QR code. Issued at first shelter contact, accepted across all providers. Digital backup survives theft or displacement.
12. Expedited government ID partnership with Service Alberta to fast-track replacement IDs for shelter residents.
13. Secure digital document locker: cloud-based storage for ID, health records, and case notes that the individual controls.
14. Service passport that tracks access across providers.
15. Mobile outreach units with integrated services, brought directly to individuals.
16. Simplified intake form shared across multiple organizations.
17. Alternative ID verification using biometric or referral-based methods.

*Community and support:*
18. Peer support network where people with lived experience guide others through the service system.
19. Post-secondary micro-credential partnerships for shelter residents.
20. Community-based service kiosks at libraries and shelters.
21. Gamified incentives for engaging with support systems.
22. Info-based portable system accessible from community centres.

**Round 2 (group convergence):** After comparing ideas, we found strong overlap in two areas: navigation tools and ID barrier reduction. Rather than choosing one over the other, we combined them. Our prototype addresses the ID barrier directly, while the broader ideation supports a vision where navigation, coordination, and access all improve together.

## Section 6: The Prototype

### What We Built

The Edmonton Shelter Network ID System is a two-part prototype combining a physical credential with a digital backend.

**Part 1: Physical ID card.** A laminated, credit-card-size card with the person's photo, name, ESN ID number, issuing shelter, and a QR code. Issued at first shelter contact. Accepted across participating organizations. If lost or stolen, the card can be reprinted in minutes because the record lives in the digital system, not on the physical card.

**Part 2: Website (ESN Connect).** A five-page prototype showing what the digital system behind the card looks like. Workers at any participating organization can scan the QR code to access a client's intake record, service history, and referral status. The site also includes an Edmonton agency directory and a funding problem explainer page that describes the structural policy barrier and what would need to change for a system like this to work at scale.

### Why This Prototype

The idea came directly from our interview with Matt. He described a cycle where people obtain government ID, experience displacement (encampment clearance, theft, violence), lose everything, and restart from zero (see also Homeless Hub, 2017; Government of Alberta, n.d.). The system does not account for this loop. Each displacement erases the documentation trail that every funded program requires.

The cycle works like this: a person arrives at a shelter without ID; the worker can provide a bed, but cannot open a formal file; without a file, the person cannot receive referrals to funded mental health, housing, employment, or addictions programs; without program access, they cannot build the documentation needed to qualify for transitional or permanent housing; without housing stability, getting replacement government ID is extremely difficult; and the cycle restarts. Every displacement resets the clock.

This is not a technology problem. A better app does not fix this. The structural problem is that government funding for social services requires documented proof of service delivery tied to identity verification. Shelters can only receive reimbursement for people they can demonstrably prove they helped, and that proof requires verifiable identity (Housing, Infrastructure and Communities Canada, n.d.). Our prototype demonstrates one mechanism for breaking the cycle, but implementing it requires policy change, not software.

### What the Prototype Tests

The technical build is simple. The real question is whether organizations would accept a non-government credential as sufficient to open a file and make referrals. That is what a real pilot would test. Our prototype makes the concept concrete enough to put in front of decision-makers and start that conversation.

## Section 7: Storyboard

[INSERT IMAGE: 6-panel storyboard, B&W, 3x2 grid]
*Figure 7. Six-panel storyboard illustrating the user experience with the ESN ID System.*

**Panel 1: The Problem.** A person arrives at a shelter after being displaced from an encampment. They have no ID. The intake worker explains: "I can give you a bed tonight, but without ID I cannot open a file, make referrals, or connect you to housing programs."

**Panel 2: First Contact.** The shelter issues an ESN ID card: a laminated card with the person's photo, name, and a QR code linked to a shared digital record. It takes five minutes. The card is accepted across all participating organizations.

**Panel 3: Navigating Services.** The person visits a mental health clinic. They present the ESN card. The clinic scans the QR code and sees their basic intake record. No need to retell their story from scratch.

**Panel 4: Building a Record.** Over the next two weeks, the person accesses employment counselling, a food bank, and a housing referral, all using the same card. Each visit is logged, building a record of engagement.

**Panel 5: Surviving Disruption.** The person's belongings are stolen. They return to any shelter in the network, verify their identity with a photo match, and receive a replacement card in minutes. The record is intact because it lives in the digital system.

**Panel 6: Outcome.** Three months later, the person has stable transitional housing. Their ESN record of consistent service engagement supported their housing application. The card did not solve homelessness. It removed the first barrier in a fragmented system and connected them to the supports that already existed.
