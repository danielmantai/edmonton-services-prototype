The Funding Problem

Why ID is required, why that traps people, and what needs to change for this system to work.

Fourth Wall Note

This page breaks the fourth wall. ESN Connect is a prototype built for RSCH3000 Applied Research at NAIT. This page explains what the real barrier is, why the prototype cannot solve it on its own, and what would actually need to change for a system like this to work. It is part of the submission, not a disclaimer buried in the footer.

1. What is the funding problem?

The most common explanation for why unhoused people without ID can't access services is that "shelters won't help them without ID." This framing is wrong, and it matters that we get it right.

Frontline workers at shelters, food banks, clinics, and housing programs want to help everyone who comes to them. The barrier is not their willingness. The barrier is structural: government funding for social services requires documented proof that a specific, identifiable person was served.

That proof requires verifiable identity. Without it, the service counts for nothing in the funding system, even if it happened.

The Core Problem

Shelters can only receive government reimbursement for people they can prove they helped. Proof requires ID. People without ID cannot be entered into funding-reportable case files. Services delivered to them are absorbed as uncompensated cost or turned away entirely. The funding model assumes everyone has government-issued ID. Many of the people most in need of services do not.

2. How the current system works — and where it fails

How funding flows

Social services in Alberta are funded through a combination of provincial grants, municipal contracts, and federal homelessness strategy money (Reaching Home). Each funding stream has its own reporting requirements, but they share a common assumption: services must be documented against an identifiable individual to be counted.

Organizations submit reports showing how many people were served, what services were provided, and for how long. These reports are the basis for continued funding. Undocumented services don't appear in the reports. Organizations that can't document their caseloads appear less productive and can lose funding.

Where ID fits in

Entering someone into a case management system typically requires: full legal name, date of birth, and government-issued photo ID (or at minimum, a document that ties the person to a verifiable identity). This isn't bureaucratic gatekeeping for its own sake. It serves real purposes: preventing duplicate records, enabling referrals between organizations, satisfying privacy regulations, and creating accountability in the funding system.

The problem is that these requirements assume a baseline level of document stability that many unhoused people don't have. People lose their ID when encampments are cleared, when their belongings are stolen, when they flee violence. Getting replacement ID requires ID, creating a documented, well-known catch-22.

The cycle this creates

The ID-Funding-Exclusion Cycle
  1. Person arrives at shelter without ID.
  2. Worker can provide a bed (emergency shelter is rarely ID-gated), but cannot open a formal file.
  3. Without a file, the person cannot receive referrals to funded programs: mental health, housing, employment, addictions.
  4. Without program access, they cannot build the documentation trail needed to qualify for transitional or permanent housing.
  5. Without housing stability, obtaining replacement government ID is extremely difficult.
  6. The cycle restarts. Every displacement resets the clock.

This is not a technology problem.

A better app does not fix this. The ESN ID card demonstrates the concept. The real barrier is structural: the funding model does not accommodate people without government-issued ID. Solving it requires policy change, not software.

3. What would need to change

For a shelter-network ID system to function at scale, changes would need to happen at multiple levels simultaneously. Technical infrastructure is the easiest part. Policy is the hard part.

Provincial policy change

The key change: Alberta's funding frameworks must recognize a network-issued credential (such as the ESN card) as a valid basis for a funding-reportable service record. This requires Service Alberta and Alberta Human Services to amend their reporting standards.

Without this, organizations that accept the ESN card still cannot claim those services in their funding reports. The card helps individuals navigate the system, but the financial barrier to organizations remains.

Organizational memorandum of understanding

Participating organizations need a formal agreement covering: which shelters accept the ESN card, the intake standard required to issue one, how the shared database is governed, and how disputes are resolved. Homeward Trust Edmonton is the natural body to coordinate this.

Data governance and privacy

A shared client database involves significant personal information. It must comply with Alberta's FOIP Act. Individuals must consent to record creation and cross-organization sharing. Governance must be independent, not controlled by any single shelter.

Technical infrastructure

The least difficult piece. A shared database requires hosting, maintenance, and security. Mobile-friendly interfaces are needed for field use. Funding could come through Homeward Trust or federal Reaching Home grants.

4. The argument this prototype makes

Daniel's Position

The funding model needs to change to allow shelter staff to vouch for unhoused people to access services, even without government-issued ID. The ESN card is one mechanism for this: a network-issued credential that serves as proof of identity and service engagement within the shelter system. Funding accountability should follow the person, not require the person to have pre-existing documentation before they can be helped.

The current model treats ID as a precondition for services. This gets the causality backwards. For many people, access to services is the pathway to eventually obtaining stable ID, not the other way around. A shelter-issued credential, backed by a photo and a digital record, provides enough identity continuity to serve the practical purposes that funding documentation requires, without demanding a government-issued document that many people genuinely cannot obtain on their own.

Shelter workers see the person in front of them. They can vouch for their identity across visits. They can confirm that services were delivered to a real individual. The funding system should allow that professional attestation to count, especially at the emergency services level where the alternative is turning someone away.

This is not an argument for eliminating accountability. It is an argument for accountability mechanisms that do not require people to already have what they are trying to obtain.

5. What this prototype demonstrates

  • That a shelter-issued digital ID system is technically straightforward to build.
  • That a shared client record (photo, intake date, service history) can persist across organizational boundaries using simple, inexpensive technology.
  • That frontline workers can create, look up, and update records without technical expertise.
  • That the ID card itself is a meaningful physical artifact, not just a database entry. It gives the person something to carry and present.
  • That card loss does not reset the record. The digital record survives displacement.

6. What this prototype does NOT do

  • It does not store real data. All client records in this demo are fictional. Nothing is transmitted to a server.
  • It does not solve the funding problem. The prototype demonstrates the concept. The funding policy change is what actually matters, and that requires government action.
  • It does not replace government ID. The ESN card is a network credential, not a legal identity document. It cannot be used to open a bank account or apply for government benefits that require photo ID.
  • It is not production-ready. This is a front-end prototype with no backend, no real authentication, no real database, and no security model. Building a real system would require significant development work and privacy compliance review.
  • It does not represent the full scope of what a real system would need. Real systems would require offline capability (for field use), multilingual interfaces, accessibility compliance, integration with existing shelter management software, and a governance structure.

7. What would need to happen next

In rough order of what matters most:

  1. Policy advocacy. Present this concept to Homeward Trust Edmonton, the City of Edmonton's Integrated Services to End Homelessness (ISEH) team, and relevant provincial MLAs. The technical prototype is the conversation starter, not the end goal.
  2. Pilot with willing organizations. A small group of shelters and service providers could agree informally to accept the ESN card for internal referrals, even before provincial policy changes. This builds a case study.
  3. Governance structure. Form a working group with representatives from shelters, Homeward Trust, and the people the system would serve. Community input from people with lived experience of homelessness is essential.
  4. Privacy and legal review. Engage with the Alberta Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner before building any real shared database.
  5. Technical build. Only after the above. The actual software is the easiest part of this problem.
The Bottom Line

Building this website took a few hours. Changing the funding model will take years of policy work, coalition building, and advocacy. That gap is not a reason to dismiss the idea. It is the reason to start making the argument clearly, with a concrete demonstration of what the solution could look like.

Research Context (RSCH3000)

This prototype was developed as part of the Week 10-11 prototyping assignment in RSCH3000 Applied Research at NAIT. The design thinking process that led here included:

  • A stakeholder interview with Matt, an inner-city outreach worker, who described the ID disruption cycle firsthand.
  • Systems mapping (iceberg and causal loop diagrams) identifying ID loss as a high-leverage point in the homelessness service system.
  • A Gold Mine Matrix ranking interventions by value and feasibility. The ESN card scored high on both.
  • A Point of View madlib: "A person experiencing homelessness needs a way to maintain a continuous identity within the service system, because displacement erases the documentation trail required to access funded programs."
  • Six-panel storyboard illustrating the user journey from first contact to stable housing.

The prototype is a low-fidelity demonstration of the digital component (the worker portal). The physical component (a laminated ESN ID card) was also built as part of the prototype package.
